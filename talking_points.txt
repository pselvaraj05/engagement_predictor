TALKING POINTS — Engagement Prediction Deck
=============================================


SLIDE 1: Title
---------------
- We built a suite of ML models to understand and predict engagement on LinkedIn shares — not just how much engagement, but who engages and what the audience mix looks like.
- This work spans three complementary models that answer different business questions about content performance.
- Everything is built on real LinkedIn engagement data: 52 million engagements across 4.8 million shares.


SLIDE 2: Problem Overview
--------------------------
- Model 1 answers "how many engagements will this get?" — it's the volume predictor, useful for identifying which posts will go viral.
- Model 2 flips the question to "who will engage?" — given a share, it predicts the job role, industry, and company of the person likely to engage.
- Model 3 is the most actionable for content strategy — instead of predicting one individual, it predicts the full audience breakdown, like "this post will attract 40% marketing, 30% sales."
- Each model builds on the same feature pipeline but frames the prediction task differently depending on the business need.


SLIDE 3: Data Overview
-----------------------
- We're working with 52.4 million engagements, 4.8 million shares, and 9 million LinkedIn profiles — this is substantial scale.
- The biggest data challenge is completeness: only 7.5% of engagements link to profiles where all three targets — job role, industry, and company — are known. That takes us from 52 million down to about 3.9 million usable rows.
- We handle rare categories by collapsing anything below a frequency threshold into an "other" bucket — this prevents the model from overfitting to categories it's seen only a handful of times.
- All models use a time-based train/test split rather than random, which is critical because random splits would leak future information and inflate results.


SLIDE 4: Feature Engineering
------------------------------
- Features are shared across all three models — TF-IDF on the post text captures what the sharer wrote, content signals capture structural patterns like questions or URLs, temporal features capture when it was posted, and the sharer's own profile provides context about who's sharing.
- TF-IDF uses up to 2,000 unigram and bigram features — this captures both individual keywords and two-word phrases that signal topic and tone.
- The sharer's profile is surprisingly predictive — a VP of Sales sharing a post attracts a very different audience than an engineer sharing the same content.
- Everything gets combined into a single sparse feature matrix: one-hot encoded categoricals, scaled numerics, and TF-IDF text vectors stacked together.


SLIDE 5: Model 1 Method — Hierarchical Engagement Prediction
--------------------------------------------------------------
- This is a three-stage hierarchical model. Stage 1 predicts if this post be popular or not? Stage 2 refines that into four tiers. Stage 3 estimates the actual count.
- The hierarchical design is intentional — a flat 4-class classifier struggles because the tiers are so imbalanced. By splitting into binary decisions first, we get much better performance on the rare but important high/viral categories.
- We use stratified sampling to oversample viral posts, which are naturally rare — only about 5% of shares cross the "popular" threshold of 50 engagements.
- The count regression uses a Tweedie objective, which is designed for count data with extreme right skew — exactly what engagement counts look like.


SLIDE 6: Model 1 Results
--------------------------
- The headline number is 95.3% recall on popular post detection — we catch nearly all high-performing content, which is exactly what you want for a content strategy tool.
- ROC-AUC of 0.950 means the model has excellent discrimination between popular and non-popular posts across all probability thresholds.
- The precision tradeoff is 58% — meaning some posts predicted as popular won't actually be. But for a "don't miss the winners" use case, high recall with moderate precision is the right tradeoff.
- Tier accuracy across all four classes is 72.5%, which is strong given that this is a 4-class problem with highly imbalanced categories.
- Count MAE of 49.8 sounds large, but engagement counts range from 0 to 50,000+ — and the tier-specific regressors keep predictions within reasonable bounds for each tier.


SLIDE 7: ROC Curve — Popularity Detection
-------------------------------------------
- This ROC curve shows Stage 1 performance — the binary popular vs not popular decision. AUC of 0.950 means we're far above the random baseline diagonal.
- The curve hugs the top-left corner, which means at most operating points we get very high recall without sacrificing too much precision.
- In practice, we can pick a threshold depending on the business need — if we want to catch every potential viral post, we move right along the curve; if we want fewer false alarms, we stay left.

SLIDE 11: Model 2 Method — Engager Profile Prediction
------------------------------------------------------
- This model operates at the engagement level — each training row is a (share, engager) pair, so a share with 50 engagements generates 50 training examples.
- We train three independent classifiers because the targets have very different characteristics — company has 47 classes with strong signal, while job role has 13 broader classes that are harder to distinguish.
- The critical data engineering step is pre-filtering profiles before joining with the 52 million engagement table — this avoids out-of-memory crashes by reducing the join size upfront.
- Top-K accuracy is the right metric here because in practice, knowing the top 3 likely audience segments is just as useful as knowing the exact one — it gives content creators a clear picture of who they're reaching.


SLIDE 12: Model 2 Results
--------------------------
- Company prediction is the standout: 83% top-1 accuracy across 47 companies. The employer_client_id provides strong signal because companies tend to have consistent engagement patterns.
- Industry reaches 73% at top-3, meaning for three-quarters of engagements, the true industry is in our top 3 predictions — very useful for targeting.
- Job role is the hardest at 30% top-1, but it climbs to 71% at top-5. This makes sense — roles are inherently diverse and a "marketing" post might attract both marketers and salespeople.
- These results are from a 50K sample. Running on the full 3.9 million rows should improve accuracy further, especially for rare categories.
- The takeaway is that we can meaningfully predict the professional profile of who will engage with a piece of content.


SLIDE 13: Power Analysis Method
---------------------------------
- This answers a practical business question: if we lose access to the LinkedIn data feed and have to scrape instead, how much data do we actually need?
- The simulation uses our existing dataset as ground truth and progressively restricts how many shares and engagements per share the model can see during training.
- We test across a grid: 50 to 20,000 shares, and 1 to 10 scraped engagements per share, with 3 random trials each for confidence intervals.
- Fixed label sets are critical — we compute the category vocabulary once from the full data so that classification difficulty stays consistent. Without this, small samples collapse everything to "other" and artificially inflate accuracy.
- The initial run used a lightweight model for speed. We've updated it to match the full 500-tree configuration for realistic estimates.


SLIDE 14: Power Analysis Results
----------------------------------
- Company prediction saturates early — just 200 shares gets you to near-maximum accuracy. Companies have strong, consistent engagement patterns.
- Job role needs about 5,000 shares to plateau, and industry is the most data-hungry at 20,000 shares.
- The practical recommendation is: track at least 20,000 shares and scrape 3-5 engager profiles per share, totaling about 80,000 profile lookups.
- Important caveat: these numbers are from the 50-tree model run. The updated 500-tree run will likely show both higher accuracy and potentially different saturation points.

SLIDE 18: Model 3 Method — Audience Distribution Predictor
------------------------------------------------------------
- This is a fundamentally different framing: instead of predicting one person's profile, we predict the entire audience distribution for a share.
- It operates at the share level — one row per share, with the target being a proportion vector (e.g., 40% Marketing, 30% Sales, 20% Engineering, 10% Other).
- This is more natural and less noisy than individual classification — aggregating over all engagers smooths out the randomness of any single person engaging.
- We use multi-output regression with LightGBM, one regressor per category, then clip and re-normalize predictions to valid probability distributions.
- The evaluation uses distribution-comparison metrics: cosine similarity measures shape alignment, Jensen-Shannon divergence measures statistical distance, and top-K overlap checks if we get the dominant segments right.


SLIDE 19: Model 3 Results — Audience Distribution Predictor
-------------------------------------------------------------
- The headline: we now predict five target dimensions — job role, industry, company, job level, and country — giving a comprehensive audience profile for each share.
- Cosine similarity ranges from 0.757 to 0.796 across the original three targets — the predicted distributions closely match actual audience breakdowns. Job level and country results are TBD pending the first full run.
- Company distribution is the strongest at 0.796 cosine similarity and 79.2% top-1 match — we correctly identify the dominant company in nearly 4 out of 5 shares.
- Proportion MAE is remarkably low: 0.47% per category for company, 0.83% for industry, 3.7% for job role — the predicted percentages are very close to actual.
- You'll notice company top-3 and top-5 overlap are lower than top-1 — this is a limitation of the regressor approach, not the data. The regressor trains 192 independent models, one per company. Most companies have near-zero proportions (~0.5%), so tiny prediction errors shuffle their rankings at positions 2 through 5, making overlap worse as K increases.
- This is why we ran the classifier comparison in the next slides — the classifier approach fixes this by using softmax probabilities that are naturally ranked, achieving 72.6% top-3 for company at 20K shares versus the regressor's 26.9%.
- This was trained on 155K shares with 31K held out for testing — much larger scale than the profile predictor, because we aggregate to the share level.
- The key advantage over Model 2: instead of predicting one person, we predict the full audience mix — this is directly what content strategists need.

SLIDE: Distribution Sample Size & Model Comparison — Method
--------------------------------------------------------------
- The regressor approach (MultiOutputRegressor) trains one model per category independently — it doesn't know proportions must sum to 1, and small errors in predicted proportions easily shuffle category rankings.
- The classifier approach reframes the same problem: instead of predicting every proportion, predict which category dominates and use softmax probabilities for ranking. Same LightGBM algorithm, different framing.
- We test all five target dimensions (job role, industry, company, job level, country) at 5,000 / 10,000 / 20,000 shares with 3 random trials each, using time-based splits and identical features, to see how both approaches scale with data.
- The key question: does the classifier's ranking-optimised softmax objective beat the regressor's independent proportion estimation for top-K accuracy?


SLIDE: Distribution Predictor — Top-3 & Top-5 Results
-------------------------------------------------------
- The classifier approach should show substantially better top-3 and top-5 accuracy, especially for company and industry, because softmax directly optimises for ranking.
- The regressor's top-K overlap is limited by independent per-category predictions — small errors in absolute proportions cause wrong rankings even when the overall distribution shape is captured well.
- More shares help both approaches, but the classifier benefits more from additional data because it learns category decision boundaries rather than proportion magnitudes.
- Job level and country results will fill in once the sample analysis is re-run with the expanded target set.
- The practical recommendation: use the classifier for top-K predictions (who dominates?), keep the regressor for full distribution estimation (what's the exact audience mix?).

SLIDE: Why Job Role Is Hard to Predict
-----------------------------------------
- Job role is consistently the hardest target across all three models. The root cause is structural: job roles cross-cut industries and content topics in a way that company and industry do not.
- Company is easy because professional networks cluster by employer — a Workday share attracts Workday employees predictably. Industry is moderately easy because post content correlates with topic domain. But job role does not correlate cleanly with content — a post about leadership attracts VPs from every industry and company.
- The text features are also a limiting factor. TF-IDF treats 'pipeline generation' and 'quota attainment' as unrelated terms even though both signal a Sales audience. Sentence transformer embeddings would capture this semantic relationship and are the single highest-ROI improvement available.
- The ground truth is also noisier for job role than for company. With the minimum of 5 complete-profile engagements per share, a single engagement shifts job role proportions by 20%. Raising that floor to 15 or 20 engagements would give cleaner distributions at the cost of fewer training shares.
- On the moderate-impact side: explicitly engineering sharer role × content type interaction features would help. A VP of Sales sharing a post about CRM attracts a very different role mix than a VP of Engineering sharing the same post. That interaction is not captured in the current feature set.
- Hierarchical grouping — collapsing 25 job role classes into 8 broader functional buckets like Technical, Commercial, Marketing, and Executive — would also improve accuracy by reducing label noise within each bucket. Once the broad function is predicted, a second-stage model can refine within it.
- The bottom line: the algorithm is not the issue, and more data alone will not close the gap. The ceiling for job role prediction will rise most from better text representations and cleaner ground truth labels.


SLIDE 25: Summary & Next Steps
--------------------------------
- We now have three completed models: volume prediction with 95% recall, profile classification with up to 93% top-5 accuracy, and distribution prediction with 0.80 cosine similarity.
- The distribution predictor is the most actionable — it directly tells content creators "this post will attract 40% marketing, 30% sales" using just the text and sharer profile.
- Remaining work: run the profile predictor on the full dataset (currently 50K sample), and re-run the power analysis with the full-strength 500-tree model.
- A future improvement would be replacing TF-IDF with sentence transformer embeddings for richer text representation — this could particularly help the job role predictions.
- The bottom line: share content and sharer profile are strong predictors of both engagement volume and audience composition.
